How It Works

Three steps. Fully private. Nothing ever leaves your device — not even a hint of your real password reaches any server.

STEP 01

Type Your Password

Your password is processed entirely inside your browser using JavaScript. It is never sent over the network, never logged, and never stored — not even temporarily on a server. Complete privacy by design.

STEP 02

Instant Strength Analysis

We compute entropy, crack time at 100 billion guesses per second, and score your password across four criteria — length, numbers, symbols, and mixed case — all updating live as you type.

STEP 03

Breach Detection

Using the k-anonymity model from HaveIBeenPwned, only the first 5 characters of a SHA-1 hash are sent. Your full password never leaves your device — yet we can still tell you if it has been leaked.

CipherMeter is a 100% browser-based cybersecurity toolkit built by Group 4 of the FUD Department of Cyber Security. Whether you're a student learning about password security, a professional auditing your credentials, or someone who just wants to know if their password is safe — this guide covers everything you need to know.

Copy & Use

CipherMeter was built as a Group 4 assignment project for the Department of Cyber Security, Federal University Dutse (FUD), 200 Level. It is a free, browser-based security toolkit designed to make password security understandable and accessible to everyone.

No account is needed. No data is stored. No information is collected. Everything runs privately inside your browser — the only exception is the breach check, which sends only a 5-character anonymised hash prefix using the industry-standard k-anonymity model. Your full password never leaves your device under any circumstances.

The tool was built using only HTML, CSS, and JavaScript — no frameworks, no dependencies, no cookies. Open source, transparent, and student-built with real cybersecurity knowledge at its core.

Type any password into the checker and the tool instantly analyses it against four security requirements — updating in real time with every keystroke:

  • At least 8 characters long (12+ is recommended for strong security)
  • Contains at least one number (0–9)
  • Contains at least one symbol such as !@#$%^&*
  • Contains both uppercase and lowercase letters (mixed case)

The segmented strength bar fills progressively from Weak (1 bar) → Medium (2 bars) → Strong (3 bars) → Unbreakable (4 bars). A contextual smart tip is shown to guide your next improvement.

A session history table also logs your last 5 tested passwords (masked for safety), so you can compare different attempts without re-entering everything.

The crack time estimates how long it would take a powerful computer making 100 billion guesses per second — a realistic rate for modern GPU-based brute-force attacks — to crack your password using exhaustive search.

Entropy (measured in bits) tells you how mathematically unpredictable your password is. It is calculated based on the size of the character set you use and the length of your password. A password with 60+ bits of entropy is considered strong. Above 80 bits is exceptional. The formula is: Entropy = length × log₂(charset size).

For example, a 12-character password using lowercase, uppercase, numbers, and symbols has a charset of 94 characters, giving about 78 bits of entropy — extremely difficult to crack by any current method.

After you stop typing (with a 600ms debounce delay), the tool checks your password against the HaveIBeenPwned database of over 14 billion leaked passwords compiled from hundreds of real data breaches worldwide.

Your privacy is completely protected via the k-anonymity model: your password is hashed with SHA-1, and only the first 5 characters of that hash are sent to the API. The server returns a list of matching hashes. Your browser then checks locally if your full hash is in the list. Your actual password never travels across any network.

If your password is found in the breach database: stop using it immediately on every account where it is used. Change it to a new unique password, ideally generated by CipherMeter's passphrase generator. Consider enabling Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) on those accounts as an additional layer of protection.

The passphrase generator creates secure, memorable passwords based on random word combinations — a technique endorsed by security experts and the NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) in their password guidelines.

  • Adjust the word count slider between 2 and 6 words
  • Toggle Numbers to append a random 2-digit number at the end
  • Toggle Symbols to add a random special character
  • Toggle Capitalize to capitalise the first word
  • Click Generate to instantly produce a new passphrase with a typewriter animation
  • Click Copy to copy it to your clipboard in one tap

A 4-word passphrase like Quantum-Shield-Nova-42! has approximately 90+ bits of entropy — far stronger than most single-word passwords, yet significantly easier to remember. This is the gold standard recommended by security professionals globally.

Most people make predictable mistakes when choosing passwords. Attackers know these patterns and exploit them first:

  • Using your name, birthday, phone number, or city — personally identifiable information is always the first guess
  • Simple substitutions like P@ssw0rd — these patterns are in every attacker's dictionary
  • Reusing the same password across multiple sites — one breach exposes all your accounts
  • Using only dictionary words alone — dictionary attacks try millions of words in seconds
  • Passwords shorter than 12 characters — short passwords can be brute-forced in minutes on modern hardware
  • Sharing passwords via WhatsApp, SMS, or email — these channels are frequently monitored or intercepted

Try these examples in the checker to see the dramatic difference between password choices in real time:

  • john1990 → Weak — no symbols, no uppercase, personal information makes it instantly guessable
  • Rocket@99 → Strong — has uppercase, symbol and number but only 9 characters — could be cracked in months
  • Quantum-Shield-42! → Unbreakable — long, mixed case, symbol, number — would take millions of years to brute-force

Data Breach
When a company's user database is illegally accessed and stolen by hackers, then exposed publicly on the dark web or sold to other criminals. Major breaches affect billions of users worldwide every year.
Hash
A scrambled, one-way mathematical fingerprint of your password. A hash cannot be reversed into the original password — it is a one-way transformation. SHA-1 and SHA-256 are common hashing algorithms.
Entropy
A mathematical measure of how unpredictable a password is. More entropy means more possible combinations, making it exponentially harder to guess. Measured in bits — 60+ bits is considered secure.
2FA / MFA
Two-Factor or Multi-Factor Authentication — a second verification step beyond just a password. Even if your password is stolen, an attacker cannot access your account without the second factor (usually a phone code or authenticator app).
Credential Stuffing
A cyberattack technique where stolen username and password pairs from one breach are automatically tested against other websites. This is why reusing passwords across sites is so dangerous — one breach can expose dozens of accounts.
Brute Force
An attack that systematically tries every possible password combination until the correct one is found. Modern GPUs can attempt billions of combinations per second, making short or simple passwords trivially easy to crack.
k-Anonymity
A privacy model used by CipherMeter's breach check. Instead of sending your full password hash, only a 5-character prefix is sent. The server returns all matching hashes, and your device checks locally — your full password is never exposed.

Additionally, always change any password immediately if you receive a breach notification, if you notice unusual account activity, or if you suspect someone else may have seen your credentials.

  • Use a passphrase instead of a single complex word — longer and more memorable is always better than shorter and complex
  • Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) on every account that supports it — especially email, banking, and social media
  • Use a reputable password manager (such as Bitwarden or 1Password) to generate and store unique passwords for every site
  • Never enter passwords on public Wi-Fi without an active VPN connection — your traffic can be intercepted
  • Never share passwords via messaging apps or email — if you must, share them in person or use an encrypted sharing tool
  • Change your most critical passwords every 1–3 months and any password immediately after a suspected breach
  • Check haveibeenpwned.com regularly to monitor if your email address has appeared in any new data breaches
