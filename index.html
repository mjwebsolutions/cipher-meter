This tool was built as a Group 4 assignment project for the Department of Cyber Security, Federal University Dutse (FUD), 200 Level. It is a free, browser-based security toolkit.

No account is needed. No data is stored. Everything runs privately inside your browser.

Type any password into the checker. The tool instantly analyses it against 4 requirements:

  • At least 8 characters long
  • Contains at least one number (0–9)
  • Contains at least one symbol (!@#$%^&*)
  • Contains both uppercase and lowercase letters

The segmented bar fills from Weak → Medium → Strong → Unbreakable.

The crack time shows how long it would take a powerful computer making 100 billion guesses per second to brute-force your password.

Entropy (in bits) measures how unpredictable your password is. Higher entropy = harder to crack. Aim for 60+ bits.

After you stop typing, the tool checks your password against a database of billions of leaked passwords.

Your privacy is protected — only the first 5 characters of a scrambled hash are sent. This is the k-anonymity model. Your actual password never leaves your device.

If found, stop using that password immediately.

Can't think of a strong password? Let the generator create one:

  • Adjust the word count slider (2–6 words)
  • Toggle Numbers, Symbols, and Capitalize
  • Click Generate for a new passphrase
  • Click Copy to copy to clipboard

Passphrases like Quantum-Shield-42! are far stronger than single-word passwords.

  • Using your name, birthday, or phone number
  • Simple substitutions like P@ssw0rd — still weak!
  • Reusing the same password on multiple sites
  • Using only dictionary words alone
  • Passwords shorter than 12 characters
  • Sharing passwords via WhatsApp or email

Try these examples in the checker to see the difference:

  • john1990 → Weak — no symbols, no uppercase, uses a name and year
  • Rocket@99 → Strong — has uppercase, symbol and number but only 9 chars
  • Quantum-Shield-42! → Unbreakable — long, mixed case, symbol, number

These are the world's most commonly leaked passwords. Never use them.

Breach
When a company's user database is stolen by hackers and exposed publicly.
Hash
A scrambled, one-way fingerprint of your password. Cannot be reversed into the original.
Entropy
A measure of how unpredictable a password is. More entropy = harder to crack.
2FA
Two-Factor Authentication — a second verification step beyond just a password.
Credential Stuffing
Using leaked username/password pairs to try logging into other websites.
Brute Force
An attack that tries every possible password combination until the correct one is found.
